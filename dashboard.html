Payment Formula

Standard Mortgage Formula
\[ M = P \times \frac{r(1+r)^n}{(1+r)^n - 1} \]
Where:
\( M \) = Monthly payment
\( P \) = Principal loan amount
\( r \) = Monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12)
\( n \) = Number of payments (loan term × 12)
Simplified Approximation
\[ \text{Payment} \approx \frac{P \times r}{1 - (1 + r)^{-n}} \]
For quick estimates when \( n \) is large (30 years = 360 payments), the denominator approaches 1, simplifying to \( P \times r \).

Advanced Insights

Key Findings

Non-Linear Rate Sensitivity
The impact of interest rate changes on monthly payments follows a non-linear pattern. A 1% rate increase from 3% → 4% increases payments by ~12%, while the same increase from 6% → 7% only increases payments by ~8%. This convexity means rate hikes hurt more at lower initial rates.
Convexity Rate Sensitivity Non-linear
Down Payment Optimization
Analysis shows diminishing returns on down payments beyond 20%. Increasing from 10% → 20% reduces monthly payments by ~11%, while 20% → 30% only yields ~4% reduction. The sweet spot for balancing liquidity and affordability appears to be 15-25%.
Optimization Diminishing Returns
Loan-to-Value Thresholds
The relationship between LTV ratios and interest rates reveals clear thresholds:
  • LTV < 80%: Best rates (avg 3.5-4%)
  • 80% ≤ LTV < 90%: +0.25-0.5%
  • LTV ≥ 90%: +0.75-1.25%
This creates clear breakpoints for down payment strategy.
LTV Threshold Effects

Strategic Recommendations

1. Rate Lock Strategy
When rates are below 4%, prioritize locking in rates as the convexity effect means future increases will have disproportionately large impacts on affordability.
2. Down Payment Allocation
Aim for at least 20% down to avoid PMI and get best rates, but don't stretch beyond 25% unless you have significant excess liquidity.
3. Refinance Triggers
Monitor for rate drops of ≥0.75% from your current rate, as this typically crosses the threshold for worthwhile refinancing after accounting for closing costs.
4. Term Selection
Consider 15-year terms when rates are low (sub-4%) as the payment premium is smaller and interest savings are maximized.
